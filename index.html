Arthritis-Friendly Game of Life Spinner

An addition to the classic board game, The Game of Life, to ease the skill of turning the spinner for those who have fine motor impairment.

Problem Statement

Little tasks, like playing a board game, are sometimes the hardest for those who face dexterity disabilities, like Arthritis. Pain and movement impairment in the hands can make it very difficult for some to execute the spinning motion with their fingers required to use the spinner in The Game of Life, preventing enjoyment and ease of play.

Design Process

Over the course of a term, I collaborated with four other students to iterate through the complete design process twice—moving from empathy and ideation to prototype creation and testing.

We began by analyzing our problem space using stakeholder maps, scenario stories, and assumption identification, alongside research into existing solutions for similar accessibility challenges. This research naturally flowed into constructing Needs and Requirements tables derived from identified stakeholder values.

To translate this information into concepts, we explored various solutions to improve accessibility, such as a game piece pusher, an enlarged car game piece, and magnetized spinner movement. These initial designs were developed into low-fidelity prototypes using simple materials like cardboard, tape, and springs. Using Best-of-Class (BOC) and SWOT analysis tools, the team decided to focus on simplifying the spinner mechanism, as we identified it as the most restrictive motion required by the game.

Entering the second iteration, we utilized Quality Function Deployment (QFD), Pairwise Tables, P-Diagrams, and Morph Charts to assess how different spinner modifications met specific requirements and constraints. We then developed a second, higher-fidelity prototype, inspired by the mechanics of a salad spinner, which was modeled in SolidWorks and 3D printed. During the engineering analysis, we applied Hooke’s Law to calculate the proper spring length and stiffness required. User testing was conducted using materials that simulated the physical restrictions of arthritis to assess "Assembly Time" and general ease of use. Meeting all performance expectations, we presented our design to the teaching team and stakeholders affected by arthritis. Further refinement is currently in progress.

Assistive Key Turner

Custom-designed assistive device that enables easier key manipulation for a client with limited grip strength and mobility.

Problem Statement

Patient with Hyperextension and Arthritis in their fingers experiences difficulty and pain gripping and inserting their key into the keyhole.

Design Process

Within the UW design team, Biomechatronics e-NABLE, my team of four picked up this personalized request by a client. Working with little direction from the client, we researched typical traits of the known disability and brainstormed aligning essential requirements within our design solution. We collaborated by conducting individual rapid iteration sessions, and coming back to formally consider each idea, combing the best of each if possible.

The client requested this device be adequate for multiple key types and sizes, being able to interchange keys as needed. This means in order to secure the key inside the device, the key could not be permanently attached, nor one size uniquely fitted for. The motion of forces applied on the key while using was experimented with. The current design incorporates a hinged encapsulation of the larger handled section of a key, with an extended handle to grip. The inside will feature a stiff but moldable interior to grip the key in place, and two strong latches are on either side to secure the encapsulation around the key. As the top of the key is fitted securely in place with the enlarged handle, the rest of the key sticks out of a slot that takes the measurements of a typical key size.

The device is modeled using SolidWorks, and will be printed for testing in the near future. When completed, the product will be personally shipped to the client for use.

Canoe Crew

Fun 3D-modeled Canadian puzzle project using Solidworks.

Design Process

With the sole constraints of print time, incorporating a Canadian theme, and inclusion of at least one animal or plant, my team of five conceptualized, modeled, and printed (and re-printed!) this fun Canadian Puzzle.

It is composed of 16 individual parts that each fit in specific ways to compose the overall puzzle. Three animals, a bear, owl, and beaver, each have a differently shaped peg to fit in the hole of their respective seat. Four wheels are threaded through two axles that are secured by wave-shaped caps to create the effect that the canoe is gliding on water. Then, two paddles can be capped on either side of the canoe and rotated back and forth.

All design decisions were made with structural integrity and aesthetics in mind. Varying error tolerances were implicated depending on the type of fit and motion desired. After the initial print, one of the caps snapped which required a reprint, and multiple pieces needed extensive sanding for proper fit. Being a group project, it was essential to communicate dimensions across group members and ensure design styles were coherent. My personal contributions came from organizing initial dimensions required, and modeling the owl and paddles.
